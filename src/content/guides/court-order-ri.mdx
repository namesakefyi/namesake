## Get prepared

The court petition is a formal request to the state to legally change your name. 

To file, you will need:

A certified copy of your birth certificate.

Proof of residency showing that you have lived in Rhode Island for at least 6 months.

Records of any previous name changes, if applicable.

A completed background check from the [Bureau of Criminal Identification and Investigation](https://riag.ri.gov/about-our-office/divisions-and-units/bureau-criminal-identification-bci). This is also called a “BCI check”.

The signed and notarized Name Change Petition form.

<details>
<summary>What if I don't have a certified birth certificate?</summary>

If you were born in Rhode Island, you can get a certified birth certificate through the [Department of Health](https://health.ri.gov/vital-records), usually through your local town hall.

If you were born outside of Rhode Island, the National Center for Health Statistics maintains a list of where to [write for vital records](https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/w2w/index.htm). Follow instructions for your birth state.

Many states also allow you to order online through [VitalChek](https://www.vitalchek.com/), although this tends to be more expensive compared to coordinating directly with a state.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I was born out of state. Can I change my name in Rhode Island?</summary>

Yes! But note: you must have legal residence in Rhode Island for **at least 6 months** before submitting a legal name change petition.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What counts as proof of residency?</summary>

Most people prove their residence with a driver’s license, utility bill, or lease agreement. Make sure it includes your current legal name, your residential address, and a date.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What if I'm unhoused? How do I provide proof of residency?</summary>

If you have an online banking app like Chime, a bank statement from there can work as long as it shows an address in the city where you are filing.

You can also use a shelter’s address when filing your name change petition. Rhode Island has a [Homeless Bill Of Rights](https://rihomeless.org/hbor/) that formally says that you cannot be discriminated against for having a shelter's address on forms like this.

</details>

## Review and print documents

Review your completed forms and check for any errors.

**Do not sign forms until you are in the presence of a notary.**

Once you have found a notary, you can complete and sign the rest of the forms.

<details>
<summary>What is a notary?</summary>

A notary is someone appointed to witness the signing of important documents, like name change petitions. Basically, the notary is making sure that you are who you say you are, and that you’re signing the document voluntarily.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How does the notarization process work?</summary>

The notary will ask for your unexpired ID with a photograph and signature. Your old driver’s license, military ID, green card, or passport should work. They’ll review your unsigned court petition and then ask you to sign it. They’ll stamp the document, note the signing for their records, and that’s it!

</details>

<details>
<summary>How can I find a notary?</summary>

You can often find notaries at your local bank branch or library.

There is likely a notary public available at the Probate Court. If you want to notarize your documents at the same time you file, call ahead of time to confirm.

Lastly, Rhode Island offers a [Notary Public Lookup](https://business.sos.ri.gov/PublicNotarySearch/Home) that can help you find a nearby notary.

</details>

## Send and receive background check

For most courts, you will need to send off your background check and receive results _before_ filing the name change petition with the court. Certain courts may be able to process the background check and the court petition at the same time, so it’s good to call and check.

If you send off the background check, you should receive the response within 1–3 weeks, but can be done more quickly if you file in-person. Most people submit by mail. You can find the most up-to-date information about background checks at the website of the [Rhode Island Attorney General](https://riag.ri.gov/i-want/get-background-check).

<details>
<summary>How do I submit the background check by mail?</summary>

To get a background check by mail, you will need:

A signed and notarized Authorization of Release (included in the form download above)

A copy of one valid form of photo identification:

Valid state issued driver’s license (front and back), or

Valid state issued identification card (front and back), or

Valid United States passport

Check or money order for $5.00, payable to “BCI.” Credit cards and cash are not accepted by mail.

A return envelope stamped and addressed to you. Include this envelope _inside_ the envelope you use to send all of the documents. The office will use this envelope to send you your final background check report.

Mail all documents, payment, and the return envelope to:

**Rhode Island Office of The Attorney General**
4 Howard Ave
Cranston, RI 02920

</details>

<details>
<summary>How do I get the expedited in-person background check?</summary>

To get a background check in person, you will need to travel to Cranston. Collect your Authorization of Release (included in the form download above) and one valid photo ID:

Valid state issued driver’s license, or

Valid state issued identification card, or

Valid United States passport

Bring your documents to:

**Rhode Island Attorney General Customer Service Center
**[4 Howard Avenue
Cranston, RI 02920](https://maps.app.goo.gl/YG6D9ko4THR6QWUb6)

Hours of operation are:

Monday–Friday, 8:30 a.m.–4:30 p.m.
(Fingerprint process stops at 4:00 p.m.)
_*Closed on state holidays_

Upon arrival, check into the virtual line by using the QR code provided on signage throughout the parking lot. After getting into the virtual line, you will receive a welcome text. A second text message will let you know when the center is ready for you. Remain in your car or in the lobby until the center is ready to assist you.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What is included in the background check?</summary>

The website for the [Rhode Island Office of the Attorney General](https://riag.ri.gov/i-want/get-background-check) states:

“Your R.I. state background check will reflect fingerprint-supported arrests that resulted in a criminal conviction or equivalent or that are pending prosecution at the time of the inquiry. These include both misdemeanors and felonies. These records will include dispositions such as guilty or nolo pleas; guilty verdicts; guilty filings, not guilty filings, and deferred sentences until those dispositions are expunged or sealed by the court.”

</details>

<details>
<summary>What if I have a criminal record?</summary>

A criminal record does not prevent you from changing your name. However, you will likely need to file the name change with the Rhode Island [Superior Court](https://www.courts.ri.gov/Courts/SuperiorCourt/Pages/default.aspx) instead of your local Probate Court.

</details>

## Gather funds or waive fees

Filing costs vary from court to court, but are around $40-50 for Probate Court, and $130 for Superior Court.

<details>
<summary>What if I can't afford court fees?</summary>

Request an “Affidavit of Indigency” (fee waiver) from the court. You may have to share details like your income and number of people in your household. If approved, the court will waive your fees.

Alternatively, you can request support from the Massachusetts Transgender Political Coalition’s [Identity Document Assistance](https://www.masstpc.org/our-programs/ida-network/) network. MTPC is a non-profit which supports trans people in the New England area, and they may be able to help pay for or reimburse your court fees.

</details>

## Submit your application

Once you have your completed background check, notarized name change document, certified birth certificate, and proof of residency, bring all documents to your local Probate Court. You must file at the Probate Court which represents your address. You cannot travel to a court elsewhere in the state.

After filing, you will receive a court date. This is normally within about two weeks. Set a reminder for your court date, then return at the date and time listed. Name change hearings usually take place in the morning.

<details>
<summary>Can I seal my name change from the public?</summary>

Name change records in Rhode Island are public by default, but you can request to seal them. Most people don’t opt to do this, but it can be worth considering when domestic violence is a factor.

At the court, request and file a miscellaneous petition. For the reason, put "I request the court seal the record on this case due to [risk of domestic violence, personal safety, etc.]”

If you want additional support or guidance, or are dealing with domestic violence, reach out to [Project Weber/RENEW](https://weberrenew.org).



</details>

<details>
<summary>What do I do if I can't make my court date?</summary>

Call the court ahead of time and ask if you can be scheduled for another date.

</details>

## Appear in court

Going to court sounds intimidating, but it’s not!

On your court date, arrive on time.

Depending on the judge, they may ask you questions about why you’re changing your name. In Rhode Island, you can change your name for any reason except fraud.

The judge will either grant your name change, or tell you why they can’t.

<details>
<summary>How long should I expect to be at the courthouse?</summary>

It depends on the court.

For Providence Probate Court, they almost always do name changes first, so folks are generally in the court for a little over an hour, and most of that time is waiting for the judge to be ready.

For Superior Court, it's similar, though the waiting time can be longer since they generally deal with more complicated cases.

If you are requesting your name change record be sealed, they may see you last to ensure privacy. A lot of the time spent in court simply depends on how many other cases there are that day, but most folks can expect to be done before 2pm.

When in doubt, reach out to the court beforehand—they will be able to give you the best estimate.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I'm nervous to appear in court.</summary>

It’s totally understandable to be nervous! You don't have to list anything trans related in the petition, you can simply write "preferred name," "more aligned with current self," or any other plausible reason for a name change.

Generally, most judges will not care about the reason you're changing your name, as they see dozens and dozens of cases a day and usually want to get them done with quickly.

If you need some additional social support before appearing in court, come chat with the Namesake community [on Discord](https://namesake.fyi/chat)—we will be happy to cheer you on!

</details>

## Receive your name change

When the judge grants your name change, you will receive a name change degree. Get extra copies of this! These official court documents are necessary when updating Social Security and other places, so it’s good to have more than one copy.

Congratulations! You did it! You have legally changed your name. Post a celebration in [Discord](https://namesake.fyi/chat).
