## Get prepared

The court petition is a formal request to the state to legally change your name. You’ll file this by mail or directly with a clerk at the [Probate and Family Court](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/probate-and-family-court/locations) in your county. If approved, you’ll be mailed a certified Decree of Change of Name, which is the official and legal proof of your name.

To file, you will need:

1. A [certified copy of your birth certificate](https://www.mass.gov/ordering-a-certificate) (long-form). This is available from the Registry of Vital Records or from the city or town where you were born.
2. Past name change documents, if applicable. For example, a marriage certificate or Judgment of Divorce.

<details>
<summary>Can I change my name if I owe debt?</summary>

Yes, you can change your name even if you have debts. However, you must notify any debt collections about legal name change. You cannot change your legal name in order to evade debt collection.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I was born out of state. Can I change my name in Massachusetts?</summary>

Yes! But note: you must have legal residence in Massachusetts for **at least 6 months** before submitting a legal name change petition.

</details>

<details>
<summary>What if my birth certificate is not written in English?</summary>

Birth certificates that are not written in English must include a written translation signed by the person translating the birth certificate with a statement that they are fluent in the foreign language and English and that the translation is a true and accurate translation. The translators signature must be notarized.

</details>

## Review and print documents

Make sure everything looks accurate and complete any missing fields.

**Do not sign the petition until you are in the presence of a notary.**

To file your name change, you’ll also need a certified copy of your birth certificate, and legal records of any previous name changes.

<details>
<summary>What is an "Affidavit of Indigency"?</summary>

An Affidavit of Indigency is a fancy way of saying fee waiver. If you qualify by the state’s standards, you can complete this form to request that your court filing fees be waived.

</details>

## File documents

You’ll file this by mail or directly with a clerk at the [Probate and Family Court](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/probate-and-family-court/locations) in your county. We suggest filing in person, if you can. You can pay by bank check, money order, or credit card at the courthouse.

<details>
<summary>Do I need an appointment?</summary>

There’s no appointment appointment required; feel free to walk in! You can also mail in your paperwork. Make sure to go to the Probate and Family Court in your county. You cannot file at other locations.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Can I submit documents online?</summary>

Massachusetts does offer [e-filing](https://www.mass.gov/guides/efiling-in-the-probate-and-family-court) as a means of submitting your paperwork. However, there is an additional $22 charge, plus a credit card processing fee, and the interface used for filing is very poorly designed. Filing in person or by mail is simpler for most people, and that’s what we recommend.

</details>

## Await decision

In most cases, you will not need to appear before a judge. Typically, decisions take 2-6 weeks to arrive by mail, and that’s it! Your name is official.

<details>
<summary>What if I’m required to attend a hearing?</summary>

You will receive a notice in the mail that includes a date, time, and location where you need to appear. Bring copies of all documents with you, including any proof of service to the hearing.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I filed my name change forms awhile ago, but I haven't heard back from the court. What do I do?</summary>

Contact the [Court Service Centers](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/court-service-centers) by phone, Zoom, or in person. When you  connect with a court representative, they can help look up your case and share the status with you.

</details>
